01 — History

A brief history of the fork

The fork is older than most people assume. Its story spans millennia, crossing empires and dining tables, shifting from a tool of the elite to an object every household takes for granted.

2400 BCE
Ancient origins
Two-pronged forks appear in ancient China and Egypt, used primarily for cooking and serving meat — not eating.
~400 CE
Byzantine refinement
Byzantine royalty used small gold forks for sticky fruit. These were ornamental as much as functional, symbols of refinement and wealth.
1004 CE
Scandalous in Venice
A Byzantine princess introduced a two-pronged golden fork to Venice on her wedding day. The clergy denounced it as an insult to God's natural instruments — the fingers.
1600s
Italy leads the way
Italy became the first European country to normalise fork use at the table. Travellers returning from Italy introduced the habit — and the mockery — to England and France.
1700s
Four tines become standard
The four-tine design emerged as the dominant form. Curved tines, better suited to scooping food, replaced the earlier straight-pronged varieties.
1800s
Industrial democratisation
Mass manufacturing made forks available to all classes. Specialised variants — salad forks, fish forks, dessert forks — proliferated as dining culture formalised.
Today
Universal and ubiquitous
Roughly 150 billion plastic forks are used each year globally. Stainless steel remains the standard for reusable cutlery in most of the world.
02 — Types

A fork for every occasion

The proliferation of fork types in the 19th century was staggering. Formal Victorian table settings could include up to a dozen different forks per place setting, each designed for a specific course or food.

Dinner fork
The largest and most versatile. Four tines, broad shoulders — the workhorse of the table.
Salad fork
Slightly shorter with a wider left tine, sometimes with a notch — designed to cut through leafy greens and light ingredients.
Dessert fork
Petite and often three-tined. Narrow enough for pastry, sturdy enough for dense cakes. A pleasing fork.
Fish fork
Broad, flat tines to gently separate delicate fillets without tearing. Often features a notch for removing bones.
Oyster fork
Three short, sturdy tines with a slight curve. One of the few forks placed to the right of the plate — beside the spoons.
Carving fork
Long, two-pronged, built to anchor large cuts of meat while carving. More tool than utensil — closer to a weapon than a fork.

04 — Materials

What forks are made of

Material determines weight, feel, durability, and how long a fork lasts. The choices have expanded significantly in the modern era.

Stainless steel
The contemporary standard. Resistant to corrosion, dishwasher-safe, affordable. Most households own forks that will outlast them.
Silver
Sterling silver forks were markers of wealth for centuries. They tarnish over time and require polishing — but they age beautifully.
Bamboo
A renewable, biodegradable alternative growing in popularity. Lighter than metal, with a natural warmth that plastic can't replicate.
Plastic
Cheap, mass-produced, and largely single-use. Convenient but ecologically costly — a significant global waste problem.
Wood
Traditional and tactile. Prone to absorbing flavours and cracking over time, but beloved in artisan and craft contexts.
05 — Facts

Things worth knowing

The fork accumulates oddities the longer you look at it.

The word "fork" comes from the Latin furca, meaning pitchfork. The dining fork was seen as a direct miniaturisation of the agricultural tool.

Thomas Coryat, an eccentric English traveller, is credited with popularising the fork in England around 1611 after observing its use in Italy. He was widely mocked for it.

In Japan, forks are still considered secondary to chopsticks, and many formal meals won't include one. The fork's dominance is deeply regional.

The average person uses about three forks per day if they eat three meals. Over a lifetime, that's roughly 80,000 fork uses — from a single design that's barely changed in 300 years.
