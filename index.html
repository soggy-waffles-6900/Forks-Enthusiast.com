01 — History

A brief history of the fork

The fork is older than most people assume. Its story spans millennia, crossing empires and dining tables — shifting from a tool of the elite to an object every household takes for granted.

2400 BCE
Ancient origins
Two-pronged forks appear in ancient China and Egypt, used for cooking and serving meat — not for eating.
~400 CE
Byzantine refinement
Byzantine royalty used small gold forks for sticky fruit. Ornamental as much as functional — symbols of wealth and status.
1004 CE
Scandalous in Venice
A Byzantine princess introduced a two-pronged golden fork to Venice on her wedding day. The clergy denounced it as an insult to God's natural instruments — the fingers.
1600s
Italy leads the way
Italy became the first European country to normalise fork use at the table. Travellers returning from Italy spread the habit — and the mockery — to England and France.
1611
Thomas Coryat, fork evangelist
English traveller Thomas Coryat returned from Italy using a fork. He was ridiculed across England. He didn't care.
1700s
Four tines become standard
The four-tine design emerged as the dominant form. Curved tines replaced straight ones — better for scooping.
1800s
Industrial democratisation
Mass manufacturing made forks affordable for all classes. Specialised variants proliferated: salad, fish, dessert, oyster.
Today
Universal and ubiquitous
Roughly 150 billion plastic forks are discarded each year globally. Stainless steel remains the standard for reusable cutlery.

02 — Types

A fork for every occasion

Victorian formal table settings could include up to a dozen different forks per place setting. Each was designed for a specific course, food, or social ritual.

Dinner fork

The largest and most versatile. Four tines, broad — the workhorse of the table.

Salad fork

Shorter, with a wider left tine and sometimes a notch — designed to cut through leafy greens.

Dessert fork

Petite and often three-tined. Narrow enough for pastry, sturdy enough for dense cake.

Fish fork

Broad, flat tines to gently separate delicate fillets. Often features a notch for removing bones.

Oyster fork

Three short, sturdy tines with a slight curve. One of the few forks placed to the right of the plate.

Carving fork

Long, two-pronged. Anchors large cuts of meat while carving. More tool than utensil.

Cocktail fork

Tiny three-tined fork used for shrimp, olives, and small appetisers. Sometimes called a seafood fork.

Fondue fork

Long-handled with two sharp tines for spearing and dipping bread or meat into hot cheese or oil.

Pickle fork

Two very long, sharp tines for fishing pickles, olives, or garnishes out of jars and narrow containers.

Pastry fork

Three tines with a wider, blade-like outer edge — designed to cut through pastry without a knife.

Serving fork

Oversized two- or three-tined fork used to transfer food from a serving dish to a plate.

Chip fork

Small disposable wooden or plastic fork used for chips and fried food. Humble but essential.

04 — Materials

What forks are made of

Material determines weight, feel, durability, and lifespan. The right material also changes how a fork feels in the hand — and against the teeth.

Stainless steel

The modern standard. Corrosion-resistant, dishwasher-safe, affordable. Most households own forks that will outlast them.

Silver

Sterling silver forks were a marker of wealth for centuries. They tarnish slowly and require polishing — but they age beautifully.

Wood

Traditional and warm to hold. Prone to absorbing flavours over time, but beloved in craft and artisan contexts.

Bamboo

Renewable and biodegradable. Lighter than metal, with a natural warmth that plastic cannot replicate.

Plastic

Cheap and ubiquitous. Roughly 150 billion plastic forks are discarded globally each year — a significant waste problem.

Titanium

Lightweight, hypoallergenic, and extremely durable. Popular with outdoor and ultralight communities.

05 — Facts

Things worth knowing

The fork accumulates oddities the longer you look at it.

Latin roots

The word "fork" comes from the Latin furca, meaning pitchfork. The dining fork was seen as a direct miniaturisation of the agricultural tool.

Thomas Coryat's embarrassment

English traveller Thomas Coryat brought the fork to England in 1611 after observing its use in Italy. He was widely mocked. He persisted anyway.

Regional resistance

In Japan, forks are still secondary to chopsticks. Many formal meals won't include one. The fork's dominance is deeply Western and far from universal.

80,000 uses in a lifetime

The average person uses roughly three forks per day. Over a lifetime, that's about 80,000 fork uses — from a design that's barely changed in 300 years.

The wrong side

In continental Europe, the fork is held in the left hand throughout a meal. In America, it's transferred to the right after cutting. Both consider the other method strange.

Clergy objections

When Byzantine princess Maria Argyropoulina brought a golden fork to Venice in 1004, the local clergy condemned it as decadent. When she died of plague two years later, some called it divine punishment.

Spork: a failed compromise

The spork has existed since the 1870s. Despite over 150 years of trying, it has never replaced either the fork or the spoon. Some things cannot be combined.
